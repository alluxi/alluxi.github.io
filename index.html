A ground-breaking candidacy

Written on Jun 07, 2015

Bernie Sanders is a self-declared democratic socialist, not too much of a difference from social liberalism, and he is running for president in the US. That is just astonishing.

One of the main issues he is advocating are income and wealth inequalities. Some would go as far as to say this is his main objective.

On his campaign website, he uses the phrase “economic and political oligarchy”. A phrase we often hear in regards to Russia and their political system and distribution of wealth. He is really onto something here. The more I think about the statement, I come to realise it is very much applicable to the American society.

The middle class is going extinct, as he quite rightly so remarks. The ridiculously wealthy always replies with “someone needs to create jobs”. That is correct, even the former Norwegian Secretary of International Development, Erik Solheim, has admitted, “Someone needs to get rich before we can lift the masses”. Nota bene; Erik Solheim is a party member of the Norwegian Socialist Left Party.

Nevertheless, this is not an excuse to get filthy rich and go fighting against general prosperity. This is a key fact Bernie Sanders has noticed. After the wealthy have created the jobs, the wealth redistribution must happen.

Sanders is also talking about universal health care. I cannot agree more. The government’s objective and goal is to make its citizens prosper. This includes having access to medical services – free of charge or at least at a rate everyone can afford.

Good luck, Bernie Sanders!

Taking care of the elderly

Written on May 02, 2015

An important element when discussing what the society can do for the elderly is public pension schemes. When every worker pays a fixed percentage of the salary every month to the government, they will benefit from a guaranteed pension later on.

Those who do not have a steady job will receive the minimum pension rate from the government. This ensures everyone a dignified life of retirement. The rate must from time to time be adjusted for inflation.

Nursing homes

Providing nursing homes for the elderly ought to be a public service. The elderly must be kept busy in order for them to be happy. This can be archived by making sure those of the elderly that want it, can establish an activity club.

To lift some of the financial burden off the state and at the same time give the elderly a more dignified life, new and existing technology must be taken in use to let the elderly live home as long as they want and are able to.

Universal subsidised day-care

Written on Apr 24, 2015

Being in day-care have several advantages attached to it. For instance, the child is socialised and gets to play with other children. On top of this, when parents can work and pay their taxes, everyone wins.

The disadvantages of this welfare service is none, as long as every day-care facility is affordable. We cannot have a first class service and a second service level for those who cannot afford it.

The main goals are to get parents to obtain taxable income and get children to socialise. The last goal is especially important for children of immigrants. They need to learn the language in order to get the most out of school later on.

You could have an eligibility requirement, like being low-income and being in a job or doing job training/education, but is that necessary? The high-income families pay more in taxes and should benefit from this scheme as well. This will prevent the different service levels and act as an equaliser. That is why we need universal subsidised day-care. No strings attached.

Rehabilitation of the prison population

Written on Apr 17, 2015

Jail time must act both deterrent for the potential criminal and as a security measure for the population as a whole. The key ingredient in any prison system is rehabilitation. The inmates will be released at some point, and at that very day must they be ready to live a reformed life without crime.

To make this happen, there must be a way for the inmates to get on with their lives. The best way of doing this is to educate them while they’re in jail and get housing and a job for when they get out. If you simply release them with nothing, then the way back to crime is not far.

The criminal code must not have too lengthy sentences either. All the more time being spent behind bars is more time for overall change to happen in society. Being able to readjust to a life outside of jail is paramount. If they don’t recognise society anymore and have no clue as to how things works anymore, there is no chance of living a life without more crime.

Sentences without maximum length

When criminals fall into a pattern of crime without any seeming end to it, then they should be convicted to imprisonment without a maximum time limit. This should be used when the crime jeopardizes someone’s life, well-being or freedom and the risk of them doing it again is imminent.

There must of course be a minimum time served, but being released is assessed by a judge, with help from expert witnesses, and not something that can be jotted down in the calendar.

The death penalty

Killing people is wrong. No matter who are doing it. The only exception is self-defence, and then it should be the last resort. Most people can change. After 20 years incarcerated, change has happened – but only if the correctional system facilitates change through rehabilitation.

Few people will never change, but to accommodate those, there should be a life imprisonment. This should be used sparingly as this only applies to the very few.

Insanity

Mentally ill criminals must not be punished for their actions. They should, just like young children, not be held accountable. They may however be sentenced to mandatory psychiatric treatment. When their condition is deemed to be compatible by society, they must be released.

There is a grey area where a mentally ill person know that their action is wrong. In those cases, punishment must be considered. Being able to understand that actions have consequences is the absolute line between being too ill to be punished and having criminal intent.
