Government imposed fees

Written on Apr 11, 2015

Taxation is a measure to fund public services, but it can also serve to discourage non-favourable behaviour. Some activities, like alcohol consumption and smoking, will impose extra government spending, such as rehabilitation clinics and cancer surgeries. Other activities doesn’t impose such extra costs for the government per se, for instance environmental fees on petrol.

Is this kind of taxation okay? In a social liberal society it is. We can’t ban everything we deem inappropriate, but we can – and should – discourage it by taxation. The fees collected by this can be used to mitigate some of the negative side effects of such actions.

Taxation by fees is a way to only affect those inflicting the surcharges. However, some services with fees are quite necessary, for instance medical treatment. These fees must not be too high for people of low-income. Some countries deal with this problem by having a maximum sum for patient’s charge pear year in regards to medical services.

Notice the clear distinction of fees used for funding with a maximum yearly payment and fees used to discourage behaviour – these fees doesn’t have any limits.

When it comes to value-added tax (VAT), there must be different rates to accommodate for different types of goods and services. Food should have a lower rate than services and other goods, because of the necessity of this. The group that notices VAT the most is the poor. There must be a social safety net in place to make sure every citizen live in dignified forms.

Indirect taxation through fees are necessary in order to provide sufficient funding for the government and as a tool to discourage non-favourable behaviour, in a society where we can’t ban everything we don’t like.

Social liberalism in a nutshell

Written on Apr 07, 2015

Social liberalism is at the core about making sure everyone have the opportunity to succeed and thrive, and at the same time not holding them back. This is done by ensuring everyone have the same foundations by having a compulsory, free school system that teaches basic skills, and that becoming a skilled worker or attending at the university must be free too, just like to compulsory education.

This will benefit both the individual who receives the education, but also the corporations and industry as well. The community is not served with a system where knowledge is a privilege for the rich and wealthy.

With the same foundations in place, it’s important to make sure that there’s a safety net which catch everyone at times of unemployment, injury or otherwise. The society must be fair and open to every citizen. This will reduce crime.

Another important institution is the universal health care system, which ought to be provided at no cost. This includes subsidized prescription drugs. Not everyone can afford health services or even insurance – that is why it must be free.

Liberal socialism must not be confused with communism. The easiest explanation is that liberal socialism is capitalism with the addition of a human element. This is called a mixed economy, and its purpose is to avoid exploitation. The majority of the workforce are employed in privately owned corporations, despite the big public sector which is in place to run all the aforementioned services, like schools and health care facilities.

How does it all get funded? There is nothing like a free lunch. The tax burden must be relatively high. Being able to compete in a globalised economy is important, but wouldn’t a high tax burden scare international corporations from doing business? That depends on what kind of businesses and corporations we’re discussing.

An educated population is desirable for high tech industry. This kind of industry will make waves to other parts of the community – creating jobs elsewhere. The knowledge based economy is the future.

A society based on social liberalism believes that the community is the key to individual success – and that is social liberalism in a nutshell.
